Field Manual · v6 · the physics of this page

Every element has mass.

This site isn’t a layout on top of an effect. The words, links, and cards are physical bodies inside a single field — they carry weight, exert force, and the field pushes back. Here’s exactly how it works, with the real engine running behind every example.

The argument underneath it: I design systems where every part exerts force on the others — and the whole only works when you can feel them.

I

Substrate

What the field is made of — and how it locks to the page.

01 — Synchronization

The page and the field share one coordinate space.

Normally the DOM and a background canvas live in separate worlds. Here a registry samples every body’s getBoundingClientRect() on each animation frame and maps it onto the canvas — scaled by device pixel ratio for retina displays.

Because it re-reads every frame, the invisible force fields stay locked to the visible boxes through scroll, resize, and reflow.

canvasₓ = rect.left + rect.width / 2  ·  DPR
DOM → field
02 — Mass & damping

Particles are point-masses in a medium.

Each particle carries a velocity in pixels per second and a mass proportional to its size. Every frame its velocity is multiplied by a friction factor — the field behaves like a fluid, so momentum bleeds off instead of accumulating forever.

vₜ₊₁ = vₜ · f  (f ≈ 0.95)
m ∝ size

Damping is why the field settles into calm swarms rather than scattering. Heavier particles swing on wider arcs.

momentum decay
03 — Currents

The waves are a flow field.

The layered waves aren’t just decoration. Near a wave line, particles pick up a drag vector along the slope of the curve — the derivative of the sine — so they drift like debris carried down a river.

slope(x) = cos(x · freq + φ) · freq · amp
vₓ += slope(x) · influence

It’s why the resting field feels alive without any input: the currents are always moving.

wave slope → drift
II

Forces

What a single body can do to the matter around it.

04 — Attraction

A body can pull the field into orbit.

An attractor defines a gravity well. Within its radius the pull grows sharply as distance falls — and a tangential component bends incoming particles into stable spirals instead of letting them slingshot away.

F = (1 − d / dmax)² · S

Drag the attractor through the field and watch the swarm follow — the force is bound to the element, not the cursor.

05 — Repulsion

A body can push the field away.

A repeller is an inverted gravity well. Within its radius the force points outward, sharpening as a particle nears the core, so matter is held at arm’s length. It carves a clean void in the swarm — a negative space the rest of the field has to flow around.

F = −(1 − d / dmax)² · S

Drag the repeller through the field and watch a clearing open around it.

repeller — drag me repel force points outward — a void opens in the field
06 — Reflection

A body can be a wall.

A reflector is an axis-aligned box. When a particle crosses an edge, the velocity component perpendicular to the nearest edge is reversed and scaled by an elasticity coefficient — then the particle is projected back outside so it can never get stuck inside. A hard hit throws a brief spark at the point of contact, so the collision reads as a collision.

if vertical edge: vₓ ← −Cᵣ · vₓ
if horizontal edge: v_y ← −Cᵣ · v_y

Drag the wall into the drifting particles to deflect them.

07 — Vortex

A body can spin the field.

A vortex applies a purely tangential force — perpendicular to the line from each particle to its core. There’s no net pull inward, so matter doesn’t collapse onto it; it wheels around. A whisper of retention keeps the whirlpool coherent instead of flinging matter off.

v += (−d̂_y, d̂_x) · S · spin
spin = ±1 → clockwise / counter

Drag the vortex into the swarm — particles orbit it rather than falling in.

vortex — drag me vortex tangential force — the field circles, never collapses
08 — Stream

A body can blow a current.

A stream emits a constant force along a fixed heading — a fan, a jet, a prevailing wind. Inside its radius every particle is pushed the same direction no matter where it sits, so the swarm combs into parallel flow.

v += (cos θ, sin θ) · S · (1 − d/dmax)
θ = data-angle, in degrees

Drag the emitter across the field and watch matter streak into lanes downwind.

stream — drag me · blows → stream directional current — particles comb downwind
09 — Spring

A body can tether the field at a fixed distance.

A spring isn’t a well — it has a rest length. Matter that crowds too close is pushed out; matter that strays too far is reeled in. So instead of collapsing onto the core, the swarm settles into an orbiting shell that springs back when you disturb it — a leash, not a drain.

v += û · (d − rest) · k
d > rest → reel in · d < rest → push off

Drag the tether through the field — matter springs back to its radius.

spring — drag me spring a tether — matter holds a shell at the rest radius
10 — Viscosity

A body can thicken the medium.

A drag body neither pulls nor pushes — it bleeds momentum. Inside its radius every particle is slowed in proportion to how deep it sits, as if the field turned to honey. Alone it just calms a patch of the swarm; paired with an attractor it’s what turns a slingshot into a settled orbit.

v ← v · (1 − k)  where  k ∝ (1 − d/dmax) · S

Agitate the field, then watch the rush die inside the zone — the faster matter loses its speed first.

viscosity — move me drag a viscous zone — momentum bleeds off, the swarm settles
11 — Accretion & release

A body can hold matter — and give it back.

An absorber captures particles that reach its core. They aren’t deleted; they’re held inside it, feeding its mass, and the element visibly inflates as it fills.

When it saturates, it goes supernova: the exact particles it captured are released radially back into the field.

scale = 1 + (M / Mmax) · 0.45
conserved: captured = released

This is the rule for the whole system — nothing is conjured from thin air, and nothing vanishes. Matter only changes hands.

absorber — fills, then pops
core
mass accumulates, then releases on supernova
12 — Emission

A body can be a fountain.

An emitter is a conduit, not a source. It draws surrounding matter into its nozzle and relaunches it as a hot jet along a fixed heading — a continuous spray of particles. The field is recycled into a stream; obeying the same law as everything else, nothing is conjured.

feed: v += d̂ · S   ·   at nozzle: v = (cos θ, sin θ) · spd

Agitate to feed it, then watch the jet stream off along its heading.

III

Conditions

When a force decides to act, and on which matter.

13 — Conditional

A force can choose what it touches.

Any body can carry a condition. It stays dormant until a test passes — gated on the element being engaged, or on a property of each passing particle: its speed, or its heat. A selective force reads every free agent and acts only on the ones that match, letting the rest drift through.

when = active · fast · slow · hot · cool · scrolling
F applied iff test(particle)

The catcher below only grips hot particles. Heat the field and the excited matter is pulled in and sorted — while the calm matter passes straight through it.

IV

System

What emerges when the parts interact.

14 — Reciprocity

The field changes the element back.

This is the part most systems miss. The element bends the field — and the field’s local density bends the element in return. As particles gather on a word, it gains weight, brightness, and lift, proportional to how much has collected on it.

--d = density(body)  →  weight, glow, rise

Hover the word — it pulls the field, the field piles up, and the pile makes the word heavier. A closed loop.

density → weight density gathered particles raise the element’s weight
15 — Threads

Engaging one body wires the set.

When you engage an element that belongs to a group, the field draws living threads between it and its siblings — pulses traveling the connections. A list stops being a list and shows itself as a connected system.

16 — Formations

The whole field can change its mind.

Forces act locally, one body at a time. A formation acts on every particle at once — a single global bias laid over the entire swarm. As you scrolled this page, each chapter quietly switched it; the field has been reorganizing under you the whole way down.
